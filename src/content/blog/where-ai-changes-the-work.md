Most of the AI conversation lives at two unhelpful extremes: it changes
everything, or it changes nothing. After years building machine learning that
ran at real scale, and tooling meant to make engineers faster, I have come to
trust a narrower, more boring claim. AI changes the work in specific places, and
leaves most of it untouched.

## Where it moves the needle

The wins cluster around tasks that are high-volume, expensive to staff, and
tolerant of being wrong some of the time:

- Drafting that a human will edit anyway.
- Triage and routing, where a good-enough guess beats a queue.
- Surfacing the few things worth a person's attention in a sea of data.

In each case the model is doing the part that was always tedious, and a person
still owns the part that carries risk.

## Where it mostly adds noise

The trouble starts when teams reach for AI on work whose whole value is
judgment, taste, or accountability. Those things do not compress.

> The question is rarely "can a model do this?" It is "what does it cost when the
> model is confidently wrong, and who carries that cost?"

## What this means for teams

The model is the easy part now. The hard part is the same as it has always
been: deciding what is worth doing, building a team that can tell good from
plausible, and being honest about how the work is going. AI raises the stakes on
that judgment. It does not replace it.
