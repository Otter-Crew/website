When a team brings me in, the brief is usually some version of "it works, but
nobody understands it anymore." The original authors have moved on, the diagram
is three rewrites out of date, and every change feels risky because no one can
predict its blast radius.

## The work is rarely addition

The instinct under pressure is to add: a new abstraction, a new service, a new
dashboard. Sometimes that is right. More often the system already contains the
ideas it needs, buried under accidental complexity that accreted one reasonable
decision at a time.

The work, then, is mostly subtraction and naming:

1. Find the handful of concepts the whole system actually turns on.
2. Give them honest names that match how people talk about the work.
3. Let everything that is merely incidental recede.

## What "done" looks like

You know an audit landed when a new engineer can hold the shape of the system in
their head after a week, and when the team stops being afraid of their own
codebase. The number of features did not change. The number of things you have
to keep in mind did.

That is the metric I care about, and it is the one that is hardest to fake.
