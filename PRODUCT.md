# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

The general public arriving cold: prospective consulting clients sizing up
whether this person can do the work, people who followed a link to one of the
projects, and readers who landed on a blog post. Nobody has an account and
nobody is logged in. Their context is a quick, evaluative skim, "what is this,
who's behind it, is it any good?", so the site has to earn attention in the
first screen and reward a deeper read. The primary job is to **learn, browse,
and discover**.

## Product Purpose

otter-crew is the home base for a one-person company. It does three jobs from a
single coherent identity:

1. **Consulting** — establishes credibility and gives prospective clients a
   reason (and a way) to reach out.
2. **Projects** — a showcase of the solo projects. Each project will get its
   own dedicated landing page over time, so the system has to stretch to host
   sibling pages without redesigning everything.
3. **Writing** — a blog tied to the projects and the work.

Success looks like a visitor forming an accurate, favorable impression fast,
then either making contact (consulting) or going deeper (a project page, a
post). The brand has to read as one person's work while feeling substantial
enough to trust with real engagements.

## Brand Personality

Calm and understated craft. Confident without selling, the work speaks and the
site gets out of its way. Quiet, spacious, precise. Human and direct in voice
(it's one person, not a department), never corporate, never breathless. Three
words: **calm, precise, credible.**

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS template.** Gradient hero, three identical feature cards, big
  hero-metric numbers, glassmorphism, the default AI-generated marketing look.
  This is the closest trap because the chosen lane (tech-minimal) collapses into
  it the moment craft slips. Avoid it deliberately.
- **Corporate / enterprise stiffness.** Stock photography, hollow buzzwords,
  risk-averse soullessness. The opposite of a real person's voice.
- **Trend-chasing.** Crypto-neon, brutalism-for-its-own-sake, whatever is
  trending on Dribbble this quarter. Choices should still look right in three
  years.

## Design Principles

1. **Restraint is the craft, not the absence of it.** Tech-minimal here means
   precision, generous whitespace, and a single confident accent, NOT the
   gradient-hero SaaS template. If a section looks like it could be on any
   startup's landing page, redo it.
2. **The work is the hero; the chrome stays quiet.** Projects and writing carry
   the site. Navigation, containers, and decoration recede so the substance
   leads.
3. **Made by a person, not a committee.** The voice is direct and human. Show
   real work and real words over manufactured polish; one honest sentence beats
   a paragraph of positioning.
4. **Timeless over trendy.** Favor choices that age well. Distinctiveness should
   come from typography, rhythm, and restraint rather than of-the-moment effects.
5. **One identity that scales sideways.** The system must host consulting, a
   growing set of per-project landing pages, and a blog without fragmenting.
   Build a coherent kit, not a single bespoke page.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No formal WCAG target. Hold a sensible baseline anyway: semantic HTML, keyboard
operability, and legible contrast (which the calm, high-whitespace direction
supports for free). Don't constrain design decisions around stricter
accessibility requirements beyond that.
